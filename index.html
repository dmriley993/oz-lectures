What you're seeing

A point w ∈ K and the normal cone at that point. Where you put w changes what NK(w) looks like: a single point at interior, an outward ray on smooth boundary, a wedge at corners. The two test toggles let you check whether a candidate vector η is actually in NK(w): the angle test evaluates the inner product at one probe y ∈ K, and the half-plane test shades the entire half-plane η defines.

The asymmetry between the two tests

They look symmetric (both involve ⟨η, y − w⟩ ≤ 0) but they're not equivalent. The angle test is refutational: a positive value at any single probe disproves η ∈ NK(w). But a non-positive value proves nothing — the next probe might be positive. The half-plane test is confirmational: it shows the entire half-plane defined by η, and if K sits inside it, then ⟨η, y − w⟩ ≤ 0 simultaneously for all y. The universal quantifier is what the half-plane shading enforces visually.

This is the same reason that in a maths proof, "I checked one example" doesn't establish a universal claim, while "I gave a structural argument" does. The half-plane test is the structural argument, made geometric.

What to try

  1. Switch to the polygon. Drop w at an edge midpoint, then drag it to a corner. Watch the normal cone go from a single ray to a fan of rays — that's the cone "opening up" as you approach a non-smooth feature of K.
  2. On the polygon, turn on the angle test. Start with η aligned with the edge normal (the test passes). Now rotate η a few degrees away — watch the inner product become positive at certain probes. The angle test catches the violation; the half-plane test would not show K inside the half-plane any more. Both are consistent.
  3. Switch to the Lorenz cone and drag w to the apex. The normal cone is now NK(0) = −K*: a wedge in the lower half-plane. This is the regime that makes "take-off" possible in the friction-cone interpretation — the apex is where the reaction can be zero and still admit a wide range of velocity behaviors.
  4. On the disk, drag w around the boundary. The normal cone is a single outward ray everywhere — no corners, so no fans. Compare with the polygon's behavior at corners: that's the difference between smooth and non-smooth convex sets.

The pedagogical takeaway: NK(w) is not just "the outward normal at w" — it generalizes the idea to non-smooth boundaries by becoming a set of admissible directions. The cone structure encodes the idea that as long as you push outward in some direction allowed by the local boundary geometry, you don't violate the constraint.

What you're seeing

Drag z anywhere in the plane; w = projK(z) is the closest point in K. The red residual arrow is z − w: how far you'd have to travel to leave K from w back to z. The orange ray (or wedge) is the normal cone NK(w) at the projection point. The geometric claim is that the residual always lies inside that cone.

Why the residual is normal

For smooth boundaries (disk, half-plane), this just says "the closest point on the boundary is where the line from z to w hits perpendicularly" — a fact you've known since high school. The new content is what happens at corners and edges. There, "perpendicular" isn't well-defined; the boundary has a corner. The right generalization isn't "perpendicular in some direction" but "perpendicular to every direction you could move along the boundary while staying in K." That set of "every direction" is exactly the normal cone.

The proof in one line: if the residual were outside NK(w), there would be a direction y − w (some y in K close to w) such that moving slightly toward y from w would decrease the distance to z. So w wasn't actually the closest point. Contradiction.

The De Saxcé shift, briefly

For the friction cone, naive normality ("residual is the friction reaction r") fails: during sliding, the velocity is along an edge of Kμ but the reaction is on a face of Kμ — they're not perpendicular in the standard inner product. The shift ṽ = (vt, vn+μ‖vt‖) corrects exactly this: it bends velocity space so that "r on the active face of Kμ" pairs with "ṽ on the dual face of Kμ*" via the projection identity. The toggle on the canvas shows this geometrically — turn it on and watch v get rotated to ṽ.

What to try

  1. On the polygon, drag z near a corner. Watch the residual rotate: as z crosses different "Voronoi regions" of the polygon, w jumps between corner and edge projections. The residual stays in the appropriate normal cone (single ray on edges, wedge at corners) the whole time.
  2. On the disk, drag z from far outside to right next to the boundary. The residual length shrinks smoothly to zero. There's no jump because the disk has no corners.
  3. On the Lorenz cone with the De Saxcé toggle off: drag z to a point where vn is slightly negative but vt is large. The naive projection gives w = 0 (apex) — the projection is "stuck", and the residual isn't normal to a friction face. Now turn the De Saxcé toggle on: w moves to a slanted face, residual becomes normal to it. The shift made the projection do the right physical thing.

The projection identity is the mathematical bridge between the two tabs: in the normal-cone tab you check that a vector is in NK(w); in the projection tab you discover that one vector — the residual z − w — automatically is. Every projection creates a normal-cone witness for free.

What you're seeing

A point mass falling under gravity, bouncing off a rigid floor, with restitution e = 1 (perfect rebound). The history chart shows the gap q, velocity v, and reaction impulse r over time. The reaction is zero except at impact instants, where it spikes — that spike is an impulsive reaction, not a force.

Why it has to be an impulse

The classical Newton equation m a = F + r doesn't make sense at impact: the velocity changes discontinuously (from −v to +e v), so acceleration is infinite, so the reaction "force" would have to be infinite for a single instant. That's not a function — it's a Dirac delta. The Moreau–Jean velocity-level scheme handles this by writing the dynamics in terms of integrals rather than pointwise forces:

m (v+ − v−) = ∫r dt

and treating ∫r dt as the primary unknown — an impulse, with units of momentum, not force. At smooth times the impulse equals (force × Δt) and ordinary BE applies; at impact times the impulse jumps the velocity directly. One scheme handles both regimes — no need to special-case impacts.

What to try

  1. Watch the height of successive bounces. With e = 1 they should be equal — energy conserved exactly. Look at the q trace in the history chart: peaks at constant height. (If you change e in source: peaks decay geometrically as e2k.)
  2. Pause at an impact instant. The v trace shows a vertical jump from negative to positive — that's the impulse acting in zero time. The r trace shows a spike at exactly that instant.
  3. Note that between impacts, r = 0 and the dynamics is just gravity. Moreau–Jean doesn't add fake "compliance" at the floor; the contact is rigid except at the impact event. Compare to a regularized scheme that would model the floor as a stiff spring and require much smaller time steps near impact.

The pedagogical message: nonsmooth dynamics doesn't mean small ε. It means choosing the right primary unknown (impulse, not force) and the right inclusion (velocity-level complementarity, not pointwise force balance). The cost is conceptual; the payoff is no stiffness, no smoothing parameter, exact contact.

What you're seeing

A unit mass on a spring, dragged by an applied force f, with smooth friction rε(v) = −tanh(v/ε) opposing motion. No projection, no inclusion: friction is a single-valued function of velocity, end of story. This is the friction model in most introductory ODE textbooks and in many engineering codes.

How it's solved

Fully implicit backward Euler. At each step, solve the scalar nonlinear equation

(m + h2k) vn+1 + h tanh(vn+1/ε) = vn + h(f − k xn)

via Newton iteration. The Jacobian F′(v) = (m + h2k) + (h/ε) sech2(v/ε) is bounded below by m + h2k > 0, so Newton is well-posed for every ε > 0. Small ε makes the linearisation stiff near v = 0 but doesn't break the solver — typical convergence is 4–8 iterations.

What to try

  1. Drag the block to x = 0.5, set f = 0, press Play. Watch the position trace. The block doesn't stay put — it creeps slowly back toward x = 0. Any nonzero kx produces a nonzero v, and smooth friction can't make v exactly zero.
  2. Drop ε from 0.5 to 0.02 and try again. Creep slows but doesn't vanish. As ε → 0 the smooth model formally converges to Coulomb in dynamics, but the stick regime is wrong at every finite ε — the block is never exactly at rest.
  3. Sweep f from −1.5 to 1.5 and back at ε = 0.1. The friction trace is a smooth sigmoid: no hysteresis loop, no sharp stick/slip threshold. The block responds smoothly, like a heavily damped oscillator with a velocity-dependent drag.
  4. Now jump to the next tab (Spring-slider: smooth vs nonsmooth) and toggle the regularize comparison on. Same physics, same parameters, but with the nonsmooth true-Coulomb law overlaid. The contrast at stick is the whole story.

Regularization is everywhere in engineering practice because it's easy. It works for problems where friction is just a damping mechanism — machinery, controls, soft robotics. It's wrong for problems where exact stick and hysteresis matter — geomechanics fault models, granular packing, structural seismic. The widget shows the difference; the choice is yours.

What this shows. Block on a rough floor pulled by a force f(t), velocity-level Coulomb friction r ∈ [−1, 1]. While the block is at rest, friction sits anywhere in the interval that balances spring force minus applied force. When |f − kx| crosses 1, friction saturates and the block breaks free. Drop f back to zero: the block stops where the spring force becomes small enough for friction to hold it again — generally not at x = 0. The friction gauge shows r as a needle inside the admissible interval.

What you're seeing

A unit mass on a spring, dragged left/right by an applied force f, with Coulomb friction |r| ≤ 1 against a rigid floor. The history charts show the friction r, the position x, and the velocity v over time, plus the "applied load" f−kx (the net force friction has to balance for the block to remain stuck). The Moreau-projection scheme treats friction as a multi-valued inclusion: r is whatever value in [−1, 1] makes the BE equation consistent. The regularize toggle replaces this with the smooth approximation rε = −tanh(v/ε), solving fully implicitly via Newton iteration on a scalar nonlinear equation.

Why the toggle matters

The two methods agree in the slip regime: when |v|/ε is large, tanh saturates at ±1, and the regularized friction acts essentially like Coulomb. They diverge in the stick regime. With Moreau projection, when the load |f−kx| ≤ 1, the friction takes the value exactly needed to keep v = 0; the block is locked, position frozen — that's exact static friction with hysteresis. With regularization, "stuck" means rε = −tanh(v/ε) ≈ 0, which requires v → 0 but never reaches it; the block creeps slowly back to x = 0 because tiny residual spring forces produce tiny residual velocities. Hysteresis is broken, asymptotically.

You can see this in the position chart: the solid Moreau trace stays put after slip ends; the dashed regularized trace drifts. As ε decreases the drift slows, but it never disappears. This is the central pedagogical point about regularization — it's a faithful approximation of dynamics, but it can be a wrong approximation of statics.

The implicit-BE structure

Both methods are fully implicit: the friction value at the new step depends on the new step's velocity. For Moreau, this resolves into a one-shot projection (rn+1 = clip of a known expression to [−1, 1]). For regularization, it's a nonlinear scalar equation

(m+h2k) vn+1 + h tanh(vn+1/ε) = vn + h(f−kxn)

solved by Newton iteration. The Jacobian (m+h2k) + (h/ε) sech2(v/ε) is bounded below by m+h2k > 0, so Newton is well-posed; convergence is in 4–6 iterations across the ε range. Stiffness from small ε doesn't break the implicit solver — it just makes the linearization more sensitive near zero, which Newton handles fine.

What to try

  1. Drag the block to x = 0.5, set f = 0, press Play. With Moreau: the block sits perfectly still — static friction balances the spring. With regularization on (any ε): watch the dashed traces drift slowly; the block creeps back toward x = 0.
  2. Set ε = 0.01 (small). The dashed and solid traces almost coincide; the regularized version matches Moreau closely in dynamics. But position-chart drift after slip is still nonzero — the creep is small but not gone.
  3. Set ε = 0.5 (large). The traces visibly separate. The regularized friction transitions gradually as load approaches 1; slip starts before |load| = 1, and stick is "soft" — there's no sharp threshold. Useful when you actually want viscous-like damping, wrong when you want Coulomb.
  4. Sweep f from −1.5 to 1.5 and back. The Moreau friction trace traces out a hysteresis loop: r jumps between ±1 when slip happens, sits in (−1, 1) during stick. The regularized trace is a smooth curve — no hysteresis loop, just a sigmoidal response. Smoothness has a price.

The choice of method is not just numerical taste. Moreau is right for problems where exact stick and hysteresis matter (geomechanics fault models, granular packing). Regularization is fine for problems where the friction is just a damping mechanism (machinery, robotics). The widget lets you see exactly what each one buys and gives up.

What you're seeing

A single 2D contact with W = I. Two cones share an origin: the primal cone Kμ (narrow, softorange — where reactions r live) and the polar cone Kμ* (wide, gray — where the De Saxcé shifted velocity ṽ lives). The user input is vfree (gray dashed arrow): the velocity the contact would have if no reaction acted. The iteration searches for a reaction r that makes the actual velocity v = vfree+r physically admissible — meaning ṽ(v) lands in the polar cone Kμ* with r·ṽ = 0. The trial point Tk is the unconstrained step; the projection arrow shows the constraint snapping it back onto Kμ.

The theorem driving the picture

The contact problem with Coulomb friction is the cone-complementarity system

r ∈ Kμ,   ṽ(v) ∈ Kμ*,   r · ṽ(v) = 0.

The orthogonality r·ṽ = 0 — together with the cone memberships — forces exactly one of three regimes to hold at the solution:

  1. Sticking: ṽ = 0, r in the strict interior of Kμ. Friction is below saturation; the contact is locked.
  2. Sliding: r on a slanted face of Kμ, ṽ on the dual face of Kμ*, perpendicular to each other in the inner product. Friction is saturated; relative motion happens, and its direction is fixed by which face r sits on.
  3. Take-off: r = 0, ṽ free in Kμ*. The contact has separated; no reaction.

The iteration solves this via the De Saxcé fixed-point map: at each step, compute the trial Tk = rk − ρṽk (the gradient-like step that would reduce r·ṽ if there were no constraint), then project: rk+1 = projKμ(Tk). A fixed point of this map satisfies the complementarity system above — that's why the iteration converges to a physically admissible reaction.

What to try

  1. Drag vfree into the upper half-plane (vn > 0). Run to convergence. r collapses to the origin (no red arrow); ṽ sits in the strict interior of Kμ*. Take-off: the contact has separated, and any non-negative normal velocity is admissible without a reaction. Why is there no right-angle marker? (Hint: the orthogonality r·ṽ = 0 is trivially 0 · ṽ = 0 — true but uninformative.)
  2. Drag vfree straight down (vt ≈ 0, vn = −1). Run. ṽ collapses to the apex (no blue arrow); r ends up on the centerline of Kμ (interior). Sticking: the contact is locked, friction strictly below saturation. Why is there no right-angle marker? (Same reason as above — r·ṽ = r·0 = 0 trivially.)
  3. Drag vfree diagonally (e.g. vt = 1, vn = −0.5). Run. r lands on a slanted edge of Kμ, ṽ on the corresponding edge of Kμ*, and the green right-angle marker appears. Sliding: friction saturated, relative motion in a specific direction. This is when r·ṽ = 0 is the non-trivial orthogonality — both vectors are nonzero, and their inner product vanishes because they sit on mutually-dual faces of the two cones.
  4. Cross between regimes by sweeping vfree across the horizontal axis vn = 0. Above: take-off. Below: sliding or sticking (depending on how much tangential velocity you have). Watch r jump discontinuously between the apex and an edge — but vfree moves continuously. The regime change is discontinuous in the output, not the input; this is what makes nonsmooth time-stepping schemes work without special-casing.
  5. Decrease ρ to 0.1, then increase to 0.9. At small ρ the trial Tk is near rk and convergence is slow but smooth. At large ρ the trial overshoots, projection clips heavily, and convergence may oscillate. ρ trades step size against stability — exactly like in any gradient-projection method.

What changes for general W: the trial direction is no longer aligned with −ṽ in the standard inner product, so the geometric mapping from vfree to regime warps. The cone-complementarity theorem and the iteration scheme are unchanged; only the picture distorts.
